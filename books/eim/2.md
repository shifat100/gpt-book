## 2.1 EXPORT MARKET SELECTION

### 2.1.1 Selecting the Right Export Commodity

Choosing the right product to export is perhaps the most critical decision for export success. This decision should be based on systematic analysis rather than intuition alone.

**Key Criteria for Product Selection:**

**1. Competitive Advantage**
- Does your product have unique features or quality advantages?
- Can you offer better pricing than competitors?
- Do you have proprietary technology or processes?
- What makes your product different from alternatives?

**2. Market Demand Assessment**
- Is there proven demand in international markets?
- What is the market size and growth potential?
- Are there seasonal demand patterns?
- Is demand stable or volatile?

**3. Production Capability**
- Do you have adequate production capacity?
- Can you maintain consistent quality at scale?
- Are raw materials readily available?
- Can you meet delivery timelines reliably?

**4. Regulatory Compliance**
- Does the product meet international quality standards?
- Are there complex certification requirements?
- Are there restrictions or quotas in target markets?
- Can you comply with labeling and packaging regulations?

**5. Logistics Feasibility**
- Is the product suitable for international shipping?
- What are the transportation costs relative to product value?
- Does it require special handling (refrigeration, fragile care)?
- What is the shelf life or durability?

**6. Profitability Analysis**
- What are the total costs (production, shipping, duties, marketing)?
- What is the achievable price in target markets?
- What is the expected profit margin?
- What is the break-even volume?

**7. Competition Analysis**
- Who are the existing suppliers in target markets?
- What is their market share and pricing?
- Can you differentiate your offering?
- What are barriers to entry?

### 2.1.4 Cultural Issues in International Markets

Cultural differences significantly impact business success. Understanding and respecting cultural nuances is crucial.

**A. Communication Styles**

**Language:**
- Learn basic phrases in local language
- Use professional translation services for documents
- Be aware of language nuances and idioms
- Avoid humor that may not translate well

**Communication Preferences:**
- **Direct vs. Indirect:** Western cultures tend to be direct; Asian cultures may be more indirect
- **Formal vs. Informal:** Some cultures prefer formal business communication
- **Written vs. Verbal:** Importance of documentation varies by culture

**Non-Verbal Communication:**
- Body language meanings differ across cultures
- Eye contact norms vary
- Personal space expectations differ
- Gestures may have different meanings

**B. Business Etiquette**

**Meeting Protocols:**
- Greeting customs (handshake, bow, etc.)
- Business card exchange rituals
- Seating arrangements
- Meeting structure and timing

**Negotiation Styles:**
- **Time Orientation:** Some cultures value punctuality; others are flexible
- **Decision-Making:** Individual vs. consensus-based
- **Relationship vs. Transaction:** Some cultures prioritize relationships before business

**Gift Giving:**
- When is it appropriate?
- What types of gifts are acceptable?
- How to present gifts?
- Cultural taboos to avoid

**Examples:**

| **Country/Region** | **Key Cultural Considerations** |
|-------------------|--------------------------------|
| **USA** | Direct communication, value time efficiency, individualistic, informal after introduction |
| **European Union** | Formal initially, punctuality important, quality-focused, documentation emphasis |
| **Middle East** | Relationship-building crucial, hospitality important, avoid left-hand use, respect for hierarchy |
| **China** | Relationship (Guanxi) essential, patience required, face-saving important, group harmony valued |
| **Japan** | Respect and formality, business card ritual, consensus decision-making, attention to detail |
| **India** | Relationship-focused, hierarchy respected, flexibility on time, bargaining expected |

**C. Business Practices**

**Working Hours and Holidays:**
- Standard business hours vary by country
- Weekend days differ (Friday-Saturday vs. Saturday-Sunday)
- Religious and national holidays affect business operations
- Seasonal business slowdowns (summer in Europe, Ramadan in Muslim countries)

**Payment Practices:**
- Payment term preferences
- Trust levels and documentation requirements
- Currency preferences
- Banking system efficiency

**Contract and Legal Preferences:**
- Detail level in contracts
- Enforceability of agreements
- Dispute resolution preferences
- Legal system differences

**D. Product Adaptation**

**Design and Packaging:**
- Color meanings and preferences
- Symbol interpretations
- Package size preferences
- Label language requirements

**Examples:**
- White color: Purity in Western cultures, mourning in some Asian cultures
- Numbers: 13 unlucky in West, 4 unlucky in China/Japan, 8 lucky in China
- Symbols: Owl means wisdom in West, bad luck in Middle East

**Product Specifications:**
- Size and measurement systems (metric vs. imperial)
- Voltage and technical standards
- Climate considerations
- Quality and safety standards

**Brand and Marketing:**
- Brand name pronunciation and meaning in local language
- Marketing message appropriateness
- Advertising content sensitivity
- Celebrity endorsement effectiveness

**E. Religious and Social Norms**

**Religious Considerations:**
- **Halal:** Important for Muslim markets (Middle East, Malaysia, Indonesia)
- **Kosher:** Required for some Jewish customers
- **Vegetarian:** Significant in India and among certain communities
- **Alcohol and Pork:** Prohibited in Muslim countries

**Social Values:**
- Family orientation and collectivism
- Gender roles and business interactions
- Age and hierarchy respect
- Environmental and social responsibility

**F. Strategies for Cultural Adaptation**

**Research and Learning:**
- Study target market culture before entry
- Read books and articles about the culture
- Attend cultural training programs
- Consult with country experts

**Local Partnerships:**
- Work with local agents or distributors
- Hire local staff who understand culture
- Partner with local businesses
- Seek mentorship from experienced exporters

**Flexibility and Respect:**
- Be open to different ways of doing business
- Show respect for local customs
- Adapt your approach when necessary
- Learn from mistakes without taking offense

**Continuous Adaptation:**
- Regularly gather feedback from local partners
- Monitor market responses
- Adjust strategies based on cultural learnings
- Stay updated on cultural trends

## 2.2 MODES OF ENTRY FOR EXPORT

Selecting the appropriate mode of entry significantly impacts export success, resource requirements, and control levels.

### 2.2.1 Direct Exporting

**Definition:** The manufacturer or producer directly manages the export process and sells to foreign buyers without intermediaries in the home country.

**How It Works:**
- Company handles all export activities internally
- Direct contact with foreign buyers or distributors
- Company responsible for documentation, shipping, payment collection
- May use services abroad (foreign agents, distributors)

**Advantages:**

**Greater Control:**
- Direct control over marketing and pricing
- Better brand management
- Direct customer relationships
- Quality control throughout process

**Higher Profit Margins:**
- No commission to intermediaries in Bangladesh
- Capture full export margins
- Better pricing flexibility

**Market Knowledge:**
- Direct feedback from customers
- Better understanding of market needs
- Faster response to market changes
- Build market expertise

**Better Customer Service:**
- Direct communication with buyers
- Faster problem resolution
- Customized solutions possible
- Stronger relationships

**Disadvantages:**

**Higher Investment:**
- Need dedicated export department
- Higher staff and infrastructure costs
- Marketing expense responsibility
- Financial risk on company

**Greater Complexity:**
- Must manage all export procedures
- Documentation responsibility
- Customs and logistics coordination
- Compliance management

**Market Entry Challenges:**
- Difficult to establish distribution initially
- Time-consuming to build buyer network
- Need market knowledge and expertise
- Higher market entry barriers

**Resource Intensive:**
- Requires skilled personnel
- Time commitment from management
- Working capital requirements
- Ongoing market development costs

**When to Use Direct Exporting:**

✓ When you have adequate resources and expertise
✓ For high-value or specialized products
✓ When margins justify the investment
✓ For maintaining brand control
✓ When building long-term market presence
✓ For customized products requiring close buyer interaction

**Methods of Direct Exporting:**

**A. Direct to End Users:**
- Selling directly to final consumers
- Common in B2B for industrial products
- E-commerce for consumer goods
- Requires strong marketing capabilities

**B. Through Foreign Distributors:**
- Appoint distributors in target markets
- Distributors hold inventory and resell
- They handle local marketing and distribution
- Usually exclusive arrangements for territories

**Distributor Agreement Should Include:**
- Territory coverage
- Exclusivity terms
- Minimum purchase commitments
- Pricing and payment terms
- Marketing responsibilities
- After-sales service obligations
- Termination conditions

**C. Through Foreign Agents:**
- Agents represent your company
- They don't take ownership of goods
- Earn commission on sales
- Connect you with buyers

**Agent Agreement Should Include:**
- Commission structure
- Territory coverage
- Exclusivity or non-exclusivity
- Responsibilities and authorities
- Performance expectations
- Duration and termination

**D. Through Overseas Sales Office:**
- Establish your own sales office abroad
- Highest level of market control
- Significant investment required
- Suitable for large, committed exporters

**E. E-commerce and Digital Channels:**
- Company website with e-commerce
- International marketplaces (Amazon, Alibaba)
- Social media selling
- Low initial investment, global reach

---

### 2.2.2 Indirect Exporting

**Definition:** The manufacturer sells products domestically to intermediaries who then export them. The manufacturer doesn't directly handle export activities.

**How It Works:**
- Sell to export houses or trading companies in Bangladesh
- Intermediary handles all export procedures
- Manufacturer treats it almost like domestic sale
- Intermediary deals with foreign buyers

**Advantages:**

**Low Risk and Investment:**
- Minimal export-related investment
- No need for export expertise
- Intermediary handles complexities
- Payment risk on intermediary

**Simplicity:**
- Simple domestic sale transaction
- No export documentation burden
- No need for export department
- Focus remains on production

**Quick Market Entry:**
- Immediate access to international markets
- Utilize intermediary's established networks
- No time spent on market development
- Can test export feasibility

**Flexibility:**
- Easy to start or stop exporting
- No long-term commitments to foreign markets
- Can try multiple markets simultaneously
- Low exit barriers

**Disadvantages:**

**Lower Profit Margins:**
- Intermediary takes significant margin
- Less profitable than direct exporting
- Limited pricing control
- Commission/discount to intermediary

**Limited Control:**
- No control over marketing and pricing abroad
- Brand building difficult
- Cannot control customer relationships
- Limited feedback from end markets

**Dependency:**
- Dependent on intermediary's performance
- Reliant on their market knowledge
- Limited influence on their strategies
- Risk if intermediary fails

**Limited Market Learning:**
- No direct market experience
- Limited customer insights
- Difficult to build market expertise
- Reduced competitive intelligence

**When to Use Indirect Exporting:**

✓ Limited resources or export experience
✓ Small production capacity
✓ Testing export feasibility
✓ Short-term or opportunistic exporting
✓ For commodity or standard products
✓ When domestic business is priority

**Types of Indirect Exporting:**

**A. Export Houses / Export Trading Companies:**

**Definition:** Specialized companies that buy products from manufacturers and export them.

**How They Operate:**
- Purchase products outright or on consignment
- Handle all export procedures
- Have established foreign buyer networks
- May export multiple product categories

**Typical Arrangement:**
- They place orders with you
- You supply as per their specifications
- They handle export from Bangladesh
- You receive payment domestically

**B. Merchant Exporters:**

**Definition:** Similar to export houses but typically deal in specific product categories.

**Characteristics:**
- Take title to goods
- Specialize in particular products
- Have market expertise in those products
- Handle full export process

**C. Buying Agents of Foreign Buyers:**

**Definition:** Representatives of foreign buyers operating in Bangladesh.

**How They Work:**
- Work on behalf of foreign importers/retailers
- Source products meeting buyer specifications
- Ensure quality and compliance
- Arrange shipment to foreign buyer

**Examples:**
- Buying agents of international retail chains
- Representatives of foreign importers
- Sourcing offices of multinational companies

**Payment:**
- Usually commission-based (from foreign buyer)
- May charge supplier commission also
- Sometimes fee-based services

**D. Piggyback Exporting:**

**Definition:** Using another company's export distribution channels.

**How It Works:**
- Your products sold through another exporter's channels
- Complementary but non-competing products
- Share distribution network
- Both companies benefit

**Example:**
- A furniture exporter includes your home décor items
- A garment exporter adds your accessories
- Shared logistics and buyer relationships

### 2.2.4 Choosing the Right Mode

**Decision Framework:**

**Step 1: Assess Your Resources**
- Available capital for export investment
- Management time and commitment
- Export expertise in team
- Production capacity and flexibility

**Step 2: Evaluate Product Characteristics**
- Product complexity and customization needs
- Margin levels and price sensitivity
- After-sales service requirements
- Brand importance

**Step 3: Analyze Market Factors**
- Target market accessibility
- Competition intensity
- Distribution channel structure
- Market knowledge requirements

**Step 4: Consider Strategic Objectives**
- Short-term vs. long-term goals
- Market commitment level
- Brand-building importance
- Growth ambitions

**Recommendation Matrix:**

| **Situation** | **Recommended Mode** |
|--------------|---------------------|
| New to exporting, limited resources | Indirect (Export House) |
| Testing market feasibility | Indirect (Merchant Exporter) |
| Specialized/technical products | Direct (Through Agent) |
| Committed to long-term growth | Direct (Through Distributor) |
| High-value products, strong margins | Direct (Own Sales Team) |
| Standard commodity products | Indirect or Direct (flexible) |
| Building brand internationally | Direct (Multiple channels) |

**Progressive Approach:**

Many successful exporters start with indirect exporting to gain experience and then transition to direct exporting as they grow:

**Phase 1** (Year 1): Indirect exporting through export houses
- Gain initial experience
- Understand export documentation
- Build production capacity
- Generate cash flow

**Phase 2** (Year 2-3): Mixed approach
- Continue with export houses for some markets
- Start direct exporting to accessible markets
- Appoint agents in key markets
- Build export team gradually

**Phase 3** (Year 4+): Primarily direct exporting
- Well-established foreign distribution
- Own export department
- Direct relationships with key buyers
- Consider overseas presence

**Sourcing Agents:**

**1. Business Legitimacy**

- **SGS Supplier Verification**

**Competitive Pricing:**
- Compare quotes from multiple suppliers

- Volume discounts available
- Long-term pricing agreements possible

- Incoterms offered (FOB, CIF, CFR, etc.)
- Packaging specifications
- Shipping arrangements
- Insurance coverage

**C. Supplier Due Diligence Process**

**Step 1: Initial Screening**
- □ Search online for potential suppliers
- □ Create shortlist of 5-10 suppliers
- □ Send inquiry with detailed requirements
- □ Evaluate initial responses

**Step 2: Detailed Evaluation**
- □ Request detailed company profile

- □ Check online reputation and reviews
- □ Assess communication quality

**Step 3: Sample Request**
- □ Request product samples
- □ Negotiate sample costs and shipping
- □ Evaluate samples thoroughly
- □ Test against specifications

**Step 4: Background Check**
- □ Verify company registration
- □ Contact customer references
- □ Check with commercial attaché
- □ Consider third-party verification

**Step 5: Visit (if possible)**
- □ Schedule factory visit
- □ Inspect production facilities
- □ Meet management team

**Step 6: Negotiation**

**Step 7: Trial Order**

**Step 8: Relationship Building**
- □ Maintain regular communication
- □ Provide feedback
- □ Build long-term partnership
- □ Gradually increase order volumes

**D. Red Flags to Watch For**

**Warning Signs of Unreliable Suppliers:**

⚠️ **Communication Issues:**
- Poor or delayed responses
- Evasive answers to questions
- Unwilling to provide documentation
- Pressure tactics for quick decisions

⚠️ **Business Legitimacy Concerns:**
- Cannot provide business license
- No physical address or factory

- No verifiable company information

⚠️ **Pricing Red Flags:**

- Requests unusual payment methods
- Demands full payment upfront

⚠️ **Quality Concerns:**
- Reluctant to provide samples
- Cannot demonstrate certifications
- Vague about quality control
- No quality guarantees

⚠️ **Professional Conduct:**
- Unprofessional communication
- Unrealistic promises
- No formal quotations or contracts
- Lack of transparency

### 2.4.2 Selecting Distribution Channels

Choosing the right distribution channel is crucial for market success. The channel determines how products reach end customers and impacts pricing, control, and profitability.

**A. Types of Distribution Channels**

**Channel 1: Direct to End User**

```
Manufacturer → End User/Consumer
```

**Characteristics:**
- No intermediaries involved
- Direct sales through company sales force or e-commerce
- Common for B2B industrial products or digital goods

**Advantages:**
- Maximum profit margin
- Complete control over customer relationship
- Direct market feedback
- Better customer service possible

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires significant investment in sales infrastructure
- Limited market reach initially
- High marketing and distribution costs
- Need for strong logistics capabilities

**Best For:**
- High-value industrial equipment
- Customized products
- Digital products and services
- Niche markets

---

**Channel 2: One-Level Channel**

```
Manufacturer → Retailer → End User
```

**Characteristics:**
- Single intermediary (retailer)
- Common for consumer goods
- Retailers buy and resell to consumers

**Advantages:**
- Wider market reach than direct
- Retailers handle local marketing
- Shared inventory and risk
- Retailers provide after-sales service

**Disadvantages:**
- Reduced profit margin
- Less control over pricing and positioning
- Dependency on retailer performance
- Limited direct customer feedback

**Best For:**
- Consumer products with established demand
- Products suited for retail environment
- When retailers have strong customer base

---

**Channel 3: Two-Level Channel**

```
Manufacturer → Wholesaler/Distributor → Retailer → End User
```

**Characteristics:**
- Two intermediaries involved
- Most common for consumer goods
- Distributors aggregate demand and distribute to retailers

**Advantages:**
- Extensive market coverage
- Distributors handle logistics and credit
- Can reach small retailers efficiently
- Lower marketing costs for manufacturer

**Disadvantages:**
- Lower margins due to two intermediaries
- Minimal control over final positioning
- Longer communication chain
- Delayed market feedback

**Best For:**
- Mass market consumer goods
- Wide geographic coverage needed
- Products requiring extensive distribution
- Smaller manufacturers without resources for direct distribution

---

**Channel 4: Three-Level Channel**

```
Manufacturer → National Distributor → Regional Wholesaler → Retailer → End User
```

**Characteristics:**
- Multiple intermediaries
- Used for very wide distribution
- Common in large countries with regional variations

**Advantages:**
- Widest possible market reach
- Minimal distribution investment by manufacturer
- Local market expertise at each level

**Disadvantages:**
- Lowest profit margins
- Minimal control
- Complex channel management
- Difficult to maintain brand consistency

**Best For:**
- Low-value, high-volume products
- Markets requiring extensive reach
- When manufacturer lacks distribution resources

---

**Channel 5: Online/E-commerce Channel**

```
Manufacturer → E-commerce Platform → End User
or
Manufacturer → Own Website → End User
```

**Characteristics:**
- Digital distribution channel
- Direct or through online marketplaces
- Growing rapidly

**Advantages:**
- Lower distribution costs
- Direct customer access
- Data and analytics available
- Flexible and scalable
- Can reach global customers

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires digital marketing expertise
- Logistics challenges for physical products
- Competition intense online
- Customer trust building needed

**Best For:**
- Products suitable for online purchase
- Standardized products not requiring physical evaluation
- Digitally savvy target audience
- Niche products with scattered customers

---

**B. Factors in Channel Selection**

**1. Product Characteristics**

| **Product Factor** | **Preferred Channel** |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| High value, complex | Direct or short channel |
| Perishable | Short, fast channel |
| Bulky, heavy | Direct or through specialized distributors |
| Standardized | Longer channel acceptable |
| Requiring demonstration | Direct or through trained retailers |
| After-sales service needed | Channel with service capability |

**2. Market Characteristics**

**Market Size:**
- Large market: Longer channel for reach
- Small, concentrated market: Direct or short channel

**Geographic Spread:**
- Widely dispersed: Need intermediaries
- Concentrated: Direct distribution possible

**Customer Preferences:**
- Where do customers prefer to buy?
- What channels do they trust?
- Convenience expectations

**3. Company Factors**

**Resources:**
- Financial resources for distribution investment
- Management time and expertise
- Logistics capabilities

**Control Desired:**
- High control need: Short channel
- Less concerned about control: Longer channel acceptable

**Strategic Goals:**
- Brand building: Shorter channel preferred
- Quick market penetration: Use existing channels
- Profit maximization: Balance channel costs vs. reach

**4. Competitive Factors**

**Competitor Channels:**
- Where do competitors distribute?
- Are channels saturated or available?
- Can you differentiate through channels?

**Channel Power:**
- Are distributors/retailers powerful?
- Can you negotiate favorable terms?
- Exclusivity possible?

**5. Environmental Factors**

**Infrastructure:**
- Quality of transportation and logistics
- Warehousing availability
- Technology infrastructure

**Legal Regulations:**
- Distribution restrictions
- Licensing requirements
- Import/export regulations

---

**Selecting Channel Partners:**

**Criteria for Selection:**
- Market coverage and reach
- Financial strength and stability
- Reputation and credibility
- Management quality
- Customer base
- Facilities and infrastructure
- Sales force capability
- Willingness to stock inventory
- Credit terms offered to customers

**Evaluation Process:**
- Request detailed company profile
- Check financial references
- Visit their facilities
- Talk to their current suppliers
- Assess alignment with your objectives
- Negotiate terms and expectations clearly

**Motivating Channel Partners:**

**Financial Incentives:**
- Competitive margins and commissions
- Volume-based incentives
- Promotional support and allowances
- Credit facilities

**Support Programs:**
- Training on products and sales techniques
- Marketing materials and POS displays
- Co-op advertising support
- Regular communication and updates

**Relationship Building:**
- Treat as partners, not just intermediaries
- Regular visits and interactions
- Involve in planning and strategy
- Recognize and reward performance

**Monitoring and Control:**

**Performance Metrics:**
- Sales volumes and growth
- Market coverage achieved
- Inventory levels maintained
- Customer satisfaction
- Payment discipline

**Communication:**
- Regular reporting requirements
- Periodic review meetings
- Feedback mechanisms
- Problem resolution processes

**Contract Management:**
- Clear written agreements
- Territory definitions
- Exclusivity terms if applicable
- Performance expectations
- Termination conditions

**D. Multi-Channel Strategy**

Modern exporters often use multiple channels simultaneously:

**Example Multi-Channel Approach:**
- **E-commerce:** Direct sales through own website
- **Distributors:** For traditional retail coverage
- **Large Retailers:** Direct supply to major chains
- **B2B Sales:** Direct to institutional customers

**Benefits:**
- Wider market reach
- Risk diversification
- Flexibility to adapt
- Maximize sales potential

**Challenges:**
- Channel conflict management
- Different pricing and terms
- Complex logistics
- Resource allocation

**Managing Channel Conflict:**
- Clear channel policies
- Differentiated products for different channels
- Territory separation where possible
- Fair pricing across channels
- Open communication with partners
