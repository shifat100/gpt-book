## 3.1 Introduction to Job Analysis

Job analysis serves as the foundation of nearly all human resource management activities. It is the systematic process that helps organizations understand what employees do, how they do it, and what competencies are required to perform jobs effectively. In today's rapidly changing business environment, job analysis has evolved from a static documentation exercise to a dynamic tool that supports organizational flexibility and strategic workforce planning.

---

## 3.2 Concept and Definition of Job Analysis

### 3.2.1 What is Job Analysis?

**Job analysis** is the systematic and detailed examination of jobs within an organization to identify and document the duties, responsibilities, required skills, outcomes, and work environment of a particular job.

**Key Definitions:**

- **Edwin Flippo:** "Job analysis is the process of studying and collecting information relating to the operations and responsibilities of a specific job."

- **Michael J. Jucius:** "Job analysis is the process of determining by observation and study the pertinent information relating to the nature of a specific job."

- **Dale Yoder:** "Job analysis may be defined as the process of making a complete study of the job so as to identify its components or duties for purposes such as improving methods, establishing standards of performance, and recruitment, selection, and placement."

### 3.2.2 Key Terminologies

Understanding job analysis requires familiarity with several related terms:

| **Term** | **Definition** | **Example** |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Job** | A collection of tasks, duties, and responsibilities that constitute the total work assignment of a single employee | Marketing Manager position |
| **Position** | A specific place or location where a job is performed by an individual employee | Marketing Manager in Dhaka Office |
| **Task** | A distinct work activity carried out for a specific purpose | Preparing monthly sales reports |
| **Duty** | A larger segment of work composed of several related tasks | Managing the marketing team |
| **Responsibility** | The obligation to perform assigned tasks and duties satisfactorily | Ensuring brand consistency across campaigns |
| **Job Family** | A group of related jobs that share common characteristics | All marketing positions (Marketing Manager, Marketing Executive, Marketing Analyst) |
| **Occupation** | A broad category of jobs across different organizations with similar characteristics | Marketing professionals across all industries |

**Practical Example:**

At **Unilever Bangladesh**, the **job** of "Brand Manager" consists of multiple **positions** across different product lines (Lux Brand Manager, Wheel Brand Manager). Each Brand Manager performs **tasks** such as market research, campaign planning, and budget allocation. Their primary **duty** is managing the brand portfolio, and they have the **responsibility** of achieving sales targets and maintaining brand equity.

**1. Foundation for HR Planning**
- Identifies workforce requirements and skill gaps
- Supports succession planning initiatives
- Enables strategic workforce alignment with business goals

**2. Recruitment and Selection**
- Provides clear criteria for candidate evaluation
- Helps design effective job advertisements
- Ensures person-job fit and reduces turnover

**Example:** **Grameenphone** uses detailed job analysis to identify specific technical competencies required for their network engineering positions, ensuring they recruit candidates with the right blend of telecommunications knowledge and problem-solving abilities.

**3. Training and Development**
- Identifies training needs based on job requirements
- Helps design targeted development programs
- Supports career path planning

**4. Performance Management**
- Establishes clear performance standards and expectations
- Provides objective criteria for performance evaluation
- Links individual performance to organizational goals

**5. Compensation Management**
- Enables job evaluation and pay structure design
- Ensures internal equity and external competitiveness
- Supports defensible compensation decisions

**Example:** **BRAC Bank** conducts comprehensive job analysis for all banking positions to establish a fair and competitive salary structure that reflects job complexity, required qualifications, and market rates.

**6. Legal Compliance**
- Provides documentation for employment decisions
- Supports equal employment opportunity compliance
- Defends against discrimination claims

**7. Job Design and Redesign**
- Identifies opportunities for job enrichment and enlargement
- Supports workflow optimization
- Enables organizational restructuring

**8. Health and Safety**
- Identifies workplace hazards and safety requirements
- Supports ergonomic job design
- Ensures compliance with occupational health regulations

## 3.4 The Job Analysis Process

Conducting job analysis is a systematic process that requires careful planning, execution, and documentation. The following framework provides a comprehensive approach to effective job analysis.

### 3.4.1 Information Required for Job Analysis

To conduct thorough job analysis, organizations must gather comprehensive information across multiple dimensions:

**1. Work Activities**
- Task-oriented activities (what the worker does)
- Worker-oriented activities (human behaviors performed)
- Machines, tools, and equipment used
- Materials processed and products produced
- Services rendered

**2. Work Context**
- Physical working conditions (temperature, lighting, noise levels)
- Work schedule (shifts, flexibility, travel requirements)
- Organizational and social context
- Supervision given and received

**3. Job Requirements**
- Knowledge, skills, and abilities (KSAs) required
- Educational qualifications and certifications
- Experience requirements
- Physical and mental demands
- Personal attributes and competencies

**4. Performance Standards**
- Quality expectations
- Quantity requirements
- Time standards
- Safety and compliance requirements

**5. Compensation Information**
- Salary range and benefits
- Incentive structures
- Market comparisons

**Real-World Example:**

When **Square Pharmaceuticals** analyzes the position of "Quality Control Chemist," they gather information including:
- Laboratory testing procedures and equipment operation
- Quality assurance protocols and regulatory compliance requirements
- Educational qualifications (B.Sc. or M.Sc. in Chemistry)
- Analytical thinking and attention to detail
- Physical ability to stand for extended periods
- Performance standards related to testing accuracy and efficiency

### 3.4.2 Steps in the Job Analysis Process

The job analysis process follows a logical sequence of activities:

**Step 1: Determine the Purpose and Scope**
- Identify why job analysis is being conducted
- Define which jobs will be analyzed
- Establish timeline and resource allocation
- Secure management support and commitment

**Step 2: Review Relevant Background Information**
- Study organizational charts and workflow diagrams
- Review existing job descriptions and specifications
- Examine relevant documentation (policies, procedures, reports)
- Understand the organizational context and strategic objectives

**Step 3: Select Representative Positions**
- Choose positions that represent the full range of jobs
- Consider variations across departments, shifts, and locations
- Ensure adequate sample size for reliability

**Step 4: Collect Job Analysis Data**
- Use appropriate methods (detailed in section 3.4.3)
- Gather information from multiple sources
- Document work activities, context, and requirements
- Ensure data accuracy through verification

**Step 5: Process and Analyze Information**
- Review and organize collected data
- Identify patterns and common elements
- Verify information with job incumbents and supervisors
- Resolve inconsistencies and gaps

**Step 6: Develop Job Description and Job Specification**
- Prepare comprehensive job descriptions
- Create detailed job specifications
- Draft clear, accurate, and complete documentation
- Use standardized formats and terminology

**Step 7: Review and Validation**
- Share drafts with job incumbents and supervisors
- Incorporate feedback and corrections
- Obtain management approval
- Ensure legal compliance

**Step 8: Implementation and Maintenance**
- Communicate finalized documents to stakeholders
- Integrate into HR systems and processes
- Establish review and update procedures
- Monitor changes in job content and requirements

**Practical Application:**

**Robi Axiata Limited** follows this systematic process when analyzing customer service roles:
1. They determine the need for updated job analysis due to digital transformation
2. Review existing customer service workflows and technology platforms
3. Select representative positions across different service channels (phone, chat, social media)
4. Collect data through interviews, observations, and customer interaction analysis
5. Process information to identify core competencies and performance metrics
6. Develop updated job descriptions reflecting omnichannel customer service requirements
7. Validate with supervisors and high-performing customer service representatives
8. Implement new documents and integrate with training programs

### 3.4.3 Methods of Collecting Job Analysis Data

Organizations can employ various methods to collect job analysis information. Each method has strengths and limitations, and often a combination of methods yields the most comprehensive results.

#### **1. Interview Method**

**Description:** Direct conversations with job incumbents, supervisors, and subject matter experts to gather detailed information about job duties and requirements.

**Types of Interviews:**
- **Individual interviews:** One-on-one discussions providing in-depth information
- **Group interviews:** Multiple incumbents interviewed simultaneously for efficiency
- **Supervisory interviews:** Perspectives from those who oversee the work

**Advantages:**
- Provides rich, detailed information
- Allows for clarification and follow-up questions
- Builds rapport and employee engagement
- Captures tacit knowledge and nuances

**Limitations:**
- Time-consuming and resource-intensive
- Subject to response bias and distortion
- Requires skilled interviewers
- May miss observable behaviors

**Best Used For:** Complex professional and managerial positions, new or unique jobs

**Example:** **Dutch-Bangla Bank** uses structured interviews to analyze relationship manager positions, asking specific questions about client interaction strategies, portfolio management techniques, and problem-solving approaches.

#### **2. Questionnaire Method**

**Description:** Structured surveys completed by job incumbents and supervisors to systematically gather job information.

**Types:**
- **Open-ended questionnaires:** Allow detailed narrative responses
- **Structured questionnaires:** Use checklists, rating scales, and forced-choice items
- **Hybrid questionnaires:** Combine both approaches

**Advantages:**
- Cost-effective for large numbers of employees
- Provides standardized, quantifiable data
- Can reach geographically dispersed workforce
- Allows anonymous responses

**Limitations:**
- Response rate may be low
- Quality depends on respondent literacy and motivation
- Limited opportunity for clarification
- May oversimplify complex jobs

**Best Used For:** Large-scale job analysis projects, similar jobs across locations

**Example:** **Bangladesh Garment Manufacturers and Exporters Association (BGMEA)** uses standardized questionnaires to analyze common production roles across member factories, enabling industry-wide job standardization.

#### **3. Observation Method**

**Description:** Job analyst directly observes and records job activities, working conditions, and performance requirements.

**Types:**
- **Direct observation:** Analyst watches work being performed in real-time
- **Work sampling:** Random observations at different times
- **Video recording:** Captures work activities for detailed analysis

**Advantages:**
- Provides firsthand, objective information
- Captures actual work behaviors and context
- Identifies unsafe practices or inefficiencies
- Useful for standardized, repetitive jobs

**Limitations:**
- Time-consuming for complex or varied jobs
- Observer presence may alter behavior (Hawthorne effect)
- Difficult for mental or cognitive work
- Cannot capture infrequent or emergency tasks

**Best Used For:** Manual, repetitive, short-cycle jobs; physical tasks

**Example:** **Walton Group** manufacturing facilities use systematic observation to analyze assembly line positions, documenting precise movements, time requirements, and ergonomic considerations for television and refrigerator production.

#### **4. Critical Incident Technique**

**Description:** Collection of specific examples of effective and ineffective job behaviors that are critical to job success or failure.

**Process:**
- Identify critical job requirements
- Collect specific incidents from supervisors and employees
- Document circumstances, actions, and outcomes
- Analyze patterns to identify key competencies

**Advantages:**
- Focuses on actual behaviors that matter
- Provides concrete examples for training and evaluation
- Identifies both positive and negative performance indicators
- Links job requirements to organizational outcomes

**Limitations:**
- Time-consuming to collect sufficient incidents
- Focuses on extreme behaviors, may miss routine tasks
- Relies on memory and perception
- Requires significant analytical effort

**Best Used For:** Performance-critical positions, developing behavioral competencies

**Example:** **Banglalink** collects critical incidents from customer service interactions to identify behaviors that lead to high customer satisfaction ratings versus those that generate complaints, informing their job analysis and training programs.

#### **5. Work Diary/Log Method**

**Description:** Job incumbents maintain detailed records of their activities, tasks, and time spent over a specified period.

**Components:**
- Daily activity logs
- Task frequency and duration records
- Problem and decision documentation
- Interaction tracking

**Advantages:**
- Captures complete range of activities over time
- Records irregular or infrequent tasks
- Provides incumbent perspective
- Documents actual time allocation

**Limitations:**
- Requires employee commitment and discipline
- May be incomplete or inaccurate
- Time-consuming for employees
- Disrupts normal work routine
- Potential for selective recording

**Best Used For:** Professional and managerial positions with varied responsibilities

**Example:** **Nestle Bangladesh** asks brand managers to maintain work diaries for one month, capturing strategic planning activities, meetings, market analysis, and cross-functional collaboration to understand the full scope of the role.

#### **6. Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ)**

**Description:** A standardized, structured job analysis instrument containing 194 job elements organized into six divisions.

**Six Divisions:**
1. Information input (where and how worker gets information)
2. Mental processes (reasoning, decision-making, planning)
3. Work output (physical activities, tools, devices)
4. Relationships with other persons
5. Job context (physical and social)
6. Other job characteristics (scheduling, responsibility, pace)

**Advantages:**
- Highly standardized and quantitative
- Facilitates job comparisons across organizations
- Computer-scored for efficiency
- Useful for job evaluation and compensation

**Limitations:**
- Complex and time-consuming to complete
- Requires high reading comprehension
- May not capture unique job elements
- Less descriptive than narrative methods

**Best Used For:** Large organizations with many positions, job evaluation projects

#### **7. Functional Job Analysis (FJA)**

**Description:** A systematic approach that analyzes jobs in terms of worker relationships to data, people, and things.

**Key Components:**
- What the worker does (tasks)
- Methodologies and techniques employed
- Machines, tools, and equipment used
- Materials, products, subject matter, or services
- Worker functions related to data, people, and things

**Advantages:**
- Comprehensive and structured approach
- Enables clear job comparisons
- Produces standardized job descriptions
- Links to Dictionary of Occupational Titles

**Limitations:**
- Requires specialized training
- Time-intensive process
- May be too detailed for some purposes

**Best Used For:** Government organizations, career counseling, vocational rehabilitation

#### **8. Competency-Based Analysis**

**Description:** Modern approach focusing on underlying characteristics (knowledge, skills, abilities, and other characteristics) that lead to superior performance.

**Focus Areas:**
- Core competencies (required for all jobs)
- Functional competencies (specific to job families)
- Leadership competencies (for managerial roles)
- Technical competencies (specialized knowledge and skills)

**Advantages:**
- Aligns with strategic organizational objectives
- Flexible for changing job requirements
- Supports development and career planning
- Emphasizes results and performance

**Limitations:**
- More abstract than traditional job analysis
- Requires careful competency modeling
- May lack specificity for some HR applications

**Best Used For:** Dynamic environments, leadership roles, strategic HR initiatives

**Example:** **Standard Chartered Bangladesh** uses competency-based analysis for their relationship managers, identifying competencies such as "strategic thinking," "client relationship management," "financial acumen," and "digital literacy" rather than focusing solely on specific tasks.

### 3.4.4 Selecting the Appropriate Method

The choice of job analysis method depends on several factors:

| **Factor** | **Consideration** |
|------------|-------------------|
| **Job Type** | Manual vs. cognitive, simple vs. complex |
| **Purpose** | Recruitment vs. training vs. compensation |
| **Resources** | Time, budget, and expertise available |
| **Organization Size** | Number of jobs and employees |
| **Industry Context** | Regulatory requirements, industry norms |
| **Data Quality Needs** | Level of detail and accuracy required |

**Best Practice:** Most organizations use a **combined approach**, employing multiple methods to triangulate data and ensure comprehensive, accurate job analysis information.

## 3.5 Outcomes of Job Analysis: Job Description and Job Specification

The primary tangible outputs of job analysis are the job description and job specification. These documents serve as essential references for virtually all HR activities and provide clarity for both management and employees.

### 3.5.1 Job Description (JD)

#### **Definition**

A **job description** is a written statement that describes the duties, responsibilities, working conditions, and other aspects of a specified job. It is a factual and organized statement of what the job entails and what is expected from the job holder.

#### **Purpose of Job Description**

- Provides clear understanding of job expectations
- Serves as basis for recruitment and selection
- Establishes performance standards and evaluation criteria
- Guides training and development activities
- Supports job evaluation and compensation decisions
- Clarifies reporting relationships and organizational structure
- Ensures legal compliance and documentation

#### **Contents of a Job Description**

A comprehensive job description typically includes the following components:

**1. Job Identification Section**
- **Job Title:** Concise, descriptive name of the position
- **Job Code:** Internal identification number
- **Department/Division:** Organizational placement
- **Location:** Physical work location
- **Reporting Relationship:** Immediate supervisor and subordinates
- **Date:** When job description was prepared or last updated
- **Prepared By:** Name and title of analyst
- **Approved By:** Authorizing manager or HR representative

**2. Job Summary**
- Brief overview (2-4 sentences) capturing the essence of the job
- Primary purpose and contribution to organizational objectives
- Level of authority and decision-making responsibility

**3. Essential Duties and Responsibilities**
- Detailed list of major job tasks and responsibilities
- Percentage of time allocated to each major duty
- Frequency of task performance (daily, weekly, monthly, occasionally)
- Distinguishes between essential and marginal functions
- Typically listed in order of importance or time spent

**4. Working Conditions**
- Physical environment (office, factory, field, remote)
- Work schedule (standard hours, shifts, flexibility)
- Physical demands (standing, lifting, visual requirements)
- Environmental hazards or challenges
- Travel requirements

**5. Supervision and Relationships**
- Supervisory responsibilities (number and titles of direct reports)
- Nature of supervision received
- Internal and external contacts
- Collaborative relationships

**6. Equipment and Tools**
- Machines, tools, and equipment operated
- Technology and software applications used
- Vehicles or specialized apparatus

**7. Performance Standards**
- Quality expectations
- Quantity requirements
- Timeliness and efficiency standards
- Key performance indicators (KPIs)

**8. Career Progression**
- Potential advancement opportunities
- Career path within the organization

#### **Guidelines for Drafting Effective Job Descriptions**

**1. Use Clear, Concise Language**
- Write in simple, direct sentences
- Avoid jargon, acronyms, and technical terms unless necessary
- Use present tense and active voice
- Begin each statement with an action verb (e.g., "Manages," "Analyzes," "Coordinates")

**2. Be Specific and Factual**
- Provide concrete details about tasks and responsibilities
- Include quantifiable information where possible (e.g., "Manages budget of BDT 5 million")
- Avoid vague terms like "handles various duties"

**3. Focus on Essential Functions**
- Prioritize duties critical to job success
- Include tasks performed regularly
- Distinguish essential from marginal functions for legal compliance

**4. Keep it Current**
- Review and update regularly (at least annually)
- Reflect actual job requirements, not ideal scenarios
- Adapt to changing technology and organizational needs

**5. Ensure Legal Compliance**
- Use non-discriminatory language
- Include equal opportunity statement
- Identify essential functions for ADA compliance
- Avoid age, gender, or other protected class references

**6. Maintain Consistency**
- Use standardized format across all job descriptions
- Apply consistent terminology and structure
- Ensure alignment with organizational hierarchy

**7. Make it Comprehensive Yet Readable**
- Include all important information
- Keep length manageable (typically 2-3 pages)
- Use bullet points and formatting for readability

### 3.5.2 Job Specification (JS)

#### **Definition**

A **job specification** is a written statement of the minimum qualifications, credentials, skills, abilities, and personal characteristics required to perform a job successfully. It translates the job description into human terms, specifying what kind of person is needed to fill the position.

#### **Purpose of Job Specification**

- Establishes hiring criteria and qualifications
- Guides recruitment and selection processes
- Supports training needs assessment
- Provides basis for person-job fit evaluation
- Ensures consistency in hiring decisions
- Supports legal defensibility of selection criteria

#### **Contents of a Job Specification**

A comprehensive job specification typically includes:

**1. Educational Qualifications**
- Minimum education level (secondary, bachelor's, master's, doctoral)
- Preferred fields of study or academic majors
- Professional certifications or licenses required
- Continuing education requirements

**2. Experience Requirements**
- Years of relevant work experience
- Specific industry or functional experience
- Progressive responsibility expectations
- Specialized experience (e.g., project management, international exposure)

**3. Knowledge Requirements**
- Theoretical knowledge needed for the job
- Understanding of principles, concepts, and methodologies
- Industry-specific knowledge
- Regulatory and compliance knowledge

**4. Skills and Abilities**
- **Technical Skills:** Job-specific competencies (e.g., software proficiency, statistical analysis, machinery operation)
- **Interpersonal Skills:** Communication, teamwork, negotiation, conflict resolution
- **Cognitive Skills:** Problem-solving, analytical thinking, decision-making, creativity
- **Leadership Skills:** Team building, motivation, delegation, strategic thinking (for managerial roles)

**5. Personal Attributes and Characteristics**
- Personality traits beneficial for job success
- Work style preferences
- Values and attitudes aligned with organizational culture
- Behavioral competencies

**6. Physical Requirements**
- Physical abilities necessary to perform the job
- Stamina and endurance requirements
- Sensory requirements (vision, hearing)
- Mobility and dexterity needs
- Lifting or carrying capacities

**7. Mental and Emotional Requirements**
- Stress tolerance and resilience
- Attention to detail and concentration abilities
- Emotional intelligence and maturity
- Adaptability and flexibility

**8. Language and Communication Skills**
- Language proficiency requirements (English, Bengali, others)
- Written and verbal communication abilities
- Presentation and public speaking skills

**9. Technology Proficiency**
- Computer literacy and software expertise
- Digital communication tools
- Industry-specific technology platforms
- Data analysis and reporting capabilities

#### **Guidelines for Drafting Effective Job Specifications**

**1. Base on Job Requirements, Not Ideal Candidates**
- Specify minimum qualifications actually necessary for job performance
- Avoid inflated requirements that unnecessarily restrict candidate pool
- Distinguish between "required" and "preferred" qualifications

**2. Ensure Legal Compliance**
- Avoid discriminatory criteria based on protected classes
- Establish business necessity for all requirements
- Focus on bona fide occupational qualifications (BFOQs)
- Use inclusive language

**3. Be Specific and Measurable**
- Define qualifications in clear, observable terms
- Specify proficiency levels (basic, intermediate, advanced, expert)
- Include concrete examples where helpful

**4. Align with Job Description**
- Ensure specifications match actual job duties and responsibilities
- Maintain consistency between JD and JS
- Reference specific JD elements when explaining specification requirements

**5. Consider Future Needs**
- Include competencies for potential job evolution
- Build in flexibility for organizational change
- Balance current needs with developmental potential

**6. Validate with Subject Matter Experts**
- Consult with successful job incumbents
- Obtain supervisor input on critical qualifications
- Test specifications against actual hiring outcomes

**7. Prioritize Requirements**
- Clearly distinguish "must-have" from "nice-to-have" qualifications
- Indicate relative importance of different requirements
- Allow for compensatory qualifications where appropriate

### 3.5.3 Differentiation: Job Description vs. Job Specification

While job descriptions and job specifications are closely related documents derived from job analysis, they serve distinct purposes and contain different types of information. Understanding these differences is crucial for effective HR management.

#### **Comparative Analysis: Job Description vs. Job Specification**

| **Aspect** | **Job Description (JD)** | **Job Specification (JS)** |
|------------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Primary Focus** | What the job entails | What the person needs |
| **Content** | Duties, responsibilities, tasks, working conditions | Qualifications, skills, abilities, personal attributes |
| **Orientation** | Job-oriented | Person-oriented |
| **Purpose** | Describes the job itself | Describes the ideal job holder |
| **Key Question** | "What needs to be done?" | "Who can do it?" |
| **Main Components** | Job title, summary, duties, responsibilities, relationships, conditions | Education, experience, knowledge, skills, abilities, physical and personal attributes |
| **Primary Users** | Job incumbents, supervisors, HR for job design | Recruiters, hiring managers, selection committees |
| **Time Frame** | Describes current state of the job | Describes requirements that persist over time |
| **HR Applications** | Performance management, job evaluation, compensation, job design | Recruitment, selection, hiring, placement, person-job fit |
| **Nature** | Factual and descriptive | Prescriptive and selective |
| **Measurement** | Output, tasks, and activities | Input qualifications and competencies |
| **Legal Relevance** | Establishes essential job functions for ADA compliance | Establishes bona fide occupational qualifications (BFOQs) |
| **Changeability** | Changes when job content changes | May remain stable even if some job duties evolve |
| **Example Statement** | "Manages cardiovascular product portfolio; develops marketing strategies; supervises team of 2" | "Requires Bachelor's in Pharmacy, 5 years marketing experience, advanced analytical skills" |
| **Development Basis** | Derived from analysis of work activities and responsibilities | Derived from analysis of KSAs needed to perform the work |
| **Level of Detail** | Detailed task breakdown | Qualification threshold standards |

#### **Relationship Between JD and JS**

While distinct, job descriptions and job specifications are complementary and interconnected:

**Sequential Relationship:**
1. Job analysis is conducted
2. Job description is created (documenting what the job is)
3. Job specification is derived (identifying what's needed to do the job)

**Functional Integration:**
- **For Recruitment:** JD informs the job posting; JS guides candidate screening criteria
- **For Selection:** JD provides context for assessing candidate fit; JS provides evaluation standards
- **For Performance Management:** JD establishes job expectations; JS identifies competencies to evaluate
- **For Training:** JD identifies tasks requiring training; JS reveals competency gaps to address
- **For Compensation:** JD shows job complexity and responsibilities; JS indicates required qualification level

**Practical Example:**

At **Banglalink Digital Communications Ltd.**:

**Job Description for "Network Operations Engineer"** states:
- "Monitors network performance across 10,000+ cell sites nationwide"
- "Troubleshoots and resolves network issues within defined SLA timeframes"
- "Implements network optimization strategies to improve quality of service"

**Job Specification for "Network Operations Engineer"** requires:
- "Bachelor's degree in Telecommunications, Electrical Engineering, or Computer Science"
- "3-5 years experience in mobile network operations"
- "Advanced knowledge of GSM, 3G, 4G LTE, and 5G technologies"
- "Certification in Cisco, Huawei, or Ericsson network technologies (preferred)"

Together, these documents provide a complete picture: *what the engineer does* (JD) and *what qualifications enable them to do it successfully* (JS).

## 3.6 Modern Trends: Job Analysis in a Jobless World

The traditional concept of fixed, clearly defined jobs is being challenged by rapid technological change, organizational restructuring, and evolving work arrangements. The phrase **"job analysis in a jobless world"** captures this fundamental shift in how work is organized and how human resource management must adapt.

### 3.6.1 The Concept of "Job Analysis in a Jobless World"

#### **What Does "Jobless World" Mean?**

The term "jobless world" does not mean the absence of work or employment. Rather, it describes an organizational environment where:

- **Work is organized around flexible roles rather than rigid jobs**
- **Project-based assignments replace permanent job structures**
- **Responsibilities continuously evolve and expand**
- **Cross-functional collaboration becomes the norm**
- **Employees move fluidly between different activities**
- **Job boundaries become blurred and permeable**

As management scholar **William Bridges** noted in his influential work, traditional jobs are becoming obsolete, replaced by "fields of work" that constantly shift and reorganize.

#### **From Jobs to Roles and Competencies**

**Traditional Job-Based Approach:**
- Fixed set of duties and responsibilities
- Clear job boundaries and hierarchies
- Stable, predictable work content
- Individual accountability for specific tasks
- Vertical career progression

**Modern Role and Competency-Based Approach:**
- Flexible set of responsibilities that adapt to business needs
- Fluid boundaries with emphasis on collaboration
- Dynamic, evolving work content
- Team accountability for collective outcomes
- Lateral and developmental career movements

**Practical Example:**

**Traditional Approach at a Bank (1990s-2000s):**
- "Loan Officer" position with fixed duties: process loan applications, verify documentation, conduct credit analysis, maintain customer records
- Clear boundaries: does not handle deposits, does not sell insurance products
- Evaluated on individual loan processing volume and accuracy

**Modern Approach at **Dutch-Bangla Bank** (2020s):**
- "Relationship Manager" role with evolving responsibilities: financial consultations, product cross-selling, digital banking adoption, customer experience management
- Blurred boundaries: collaborate with investment advisors, insurance partners, digital banking team, credit analysts
- Evaluated on comprehensive customer portfolio growth, digital adoption rates, and customer satisfaction scores

### 3.6.2 Reasons for the Shift Toward Roles and Competencies

Multiple converging forces are driving the transformation from traditional job analysis to more flexible approaches:

#### **1. Technological Disruption and Digital Transformation**

**Impact:**
- Automation eliminates routine tasks while creating new responsibilities
- Digital tools require continuous learning and skill updating
- Technology changes faster than traditional job descriptions can be revised
- Artificial Intelligence and machine learning reshape knowledge work

**Example:** At **bKash Limited**, the role of "Customer Service Representative" evolved dramatically with AI chatbot implementation. Representatives shifted from answering basic account queries to handling complex financial advisory discussions, fraud investigation, and merchant relationship supportâ€”all within three years, rendering traditional job descriptions obsolete.

#### **2. Organizational Restructuring and Agility**

**Impact:**
- Flatter organizational structures reduce hierarchical boundaries
- Project-based work requires temporary team configurations
- Matrix organizations demand multiple reporting relationships
- Agile methodologies emphasize adaptable teams over fixed roles

**Example:** **Pathao** operates with highly fluid team structures where engineers, product managers, and operations specialists form project squads for specific initiatives (e.g., "food delivery expansion," "fintech integration"), then reconfigure for new priorities, making traditional job definitions impractical.

#### **3. Globalization and Cross-Cultural Collaboration**

**Impact:**
- Virtual teams work across time zones and geographies
- Cultural diversity requires adaptability in work approaches
- Global standards and local customization create complexity
- Remote work arrangements eliminate traditional job contexts

**Example:** **Grameenphone's** IT division has teams collaborating with Telenor Group colleagues in Norway, Myanmar, Pakistan, and Thailand. Engineers routinely work on projects spanning multiple countries, requiring cultural intelligence and flexible work arrangements that transcend traditional job boundaries.

#### **4. Changing Employee Expectations**

**Impact:**
- Millennials and Gen Z seek variety, growth, and meaningful work
- Work-life integration demands flexible arrangements
- Employees value learning opportunities over job security
- Career mobility prioritizes skill development over position tenure

**Example:** **Daraz Bangladesh** attracts young talent by offering "rotation programs" where employees experience different functions (marketing, operations, business development) rather than being confined to single job roles, recognizing that career development matters more than fixed positions.

#### **5. Knowledge Economy and Intellectual Capital**

**Impact:**
- Value creation depends on knowledge, creativity, and innovation
- Tacit knowledge and problem-solving ability trump routine task execution
- Collaborative knowledge work cannot be easily decomposed into discrete tasks
- Expertise becomes more important than positional authority

**Example:** At **IDLC Finance Limited**, credit analysts are increasingly expected to combine traditional financial analysis with data science, regulatory compliance, relationship management, and strategic advisoryâ€”a constellation of competencies that cannot be captured in traditional task-based job descriptions.

#### **6. Regulatory and Market Volatility**

**Impact:**
- Frequent regulatory changes require rapid adaptation
- Market disruptions demand organizational pivoting
- Competitive pressure shortens strategic planning horizons
- Business model innovation necessitates workforce flexibility

**Example:** Bangladesh's mobile financial services sector faced rapid regulatory evolution regarding agent banking, wallet interoperability, and digital KYC. Companies like **Nagad** and **Rocket** required their compliance officers to continuously expand their roles beyond traditional regulatory monitoring to include policy advocacy, risk assessment, and cross-functional change management.

#### **7. Gig Economy and Alternative Work Arrangements**

**Impact:**
- Contingent workforce (freelancers, consultants, contractors) grows
- Traditional employment relationships diversify
- Project-based hiring becomes common
- Platform-mediated work challenges organizational boundaries

**Example:** **10 Minute School** leverages a network of freelance content creators, educators, and technologists for course development, making traditional employee job descriptions insufficient for workforce management. Instead, they focus on competency profiles and project deliverables.

### 3.6.3 Implications for Job Analysis

The shift from jobs to roles and competencies creates several implications for HR practice:

#### **Challenges:**

1. **Difficulty in Documentation:** Fluid responsibilities are harder to capture in static documents
2. **Performance Evaluation Complexity:** Measuring performance becomes more subjective without clear task standards
3. **Compensation Equity:** Job-based pay structures struggle with flexible roles
4. **Legal Compliance:** Employment laws and regulations are designed around traditional jobs
5. **Training Needs Assessment:** Identifying developmental requirements becomes more complex
6. **Recruitment and Selection:** Hiring criteria must balance specificity with flexibility

#### **Opportunities:**

1. **Enhanced Organizational Agility:** Workforce can adapt quickly to changing business needs
2. **Employee Development:** Broader roles provide richer learning experiences
3. **Innovation and Creativity:** Less rigid boundaries encourage creative problem-solving
4. **Talent Attraction:** Flexible, growth-oriented roles appeal to high performers
5. **Strategic Alignment:** Competency-based approaches link individual capabilities to organizational strategy
6. **Cross-Functional Collaboration:** Role fluidity promotes knowledge sharing and teamwork

### 3.6.4 Techniques to Foster Change in Job Design

Organizations employ various techniques to transition from traditional job analysis to more flexible, competency-based approaches:

#### **1. Competency Modeling**

**Description:** Systematic process of identifying, defining, and organizing the knowledge, skills, abilities, and behavioral characteristics required for effective performance.

**Components:**
- **Core Competencies:** Required for all organizational members (e.g., "customer focus," "integrity," "teamwork")
- **Functional Competencies:** Specific to job families or functions (e.g., "financial analysis" for finance roles)
- **Leadership Competencies:** For management and leadership positions (e.g., "strategic thinking," "talent development")
- **Technical Competencies:** Specialized expertise (e.g., "Java programming," "pharmaceutical regulatory knowledge")

**Implementation Process:**
1. Identify strategic organizational objectives
2. Determine critical competencies for success
3. Define competencies with behavioral indicators
4. Assess current workforce against competency model
5. Develop competency-based HR systems (selection, development, evaluation)
6. Review and update competencies regularly

**Example:** **Standard Chartered Bangladesh** developed a comprehensive competency framework with competencies like "Digital Innovation," "Risk Intelligence," "Client Centricity," and "Inclusive Leadership." Each competency includes proficiency levels (Awareness, Working Knowledge, Proficient, Expert) with specific behavioral indicators, allowing flexible application across evolving roles.

#### **2. Broad Banding**

**Description:** Compensation strategy that collapses traditional narrow salary grades into fewer, wider "bands" that encompass broader ranges of jobs and salary levels.

**Characteristics:**
- Fewer hierarchical levels
- Wider salary ranges within each band
- Greater flexibility in compensation decisions
- Emphasis on horizontal career development

**Benefits:**
- Facilitates lateral moves and role flexibility
- Supports organizational flattening
- Encourages skill development over title progression
- Simplifies administration

**Example:** **BRAC** implemented broad banding across its diverse operations (microfinance, education, health, agriculture), creating five broad bands (Professional, Specialist, Manager, Senior Manager, Executive) instead of fifteen narrow grades. This enables program managers to move between different development initiatives (e.g., from education to health programs) without rigid job-to-job comparisons.

#### **3. Job Rotation and Cross-Training**

**Description:** Systematic movement of employees between different jobs, functions, or departments to build diverse skills and organizational understanding.

**Types:**
- **Functional Rotation:** Movement within same department (e.g., marketing research to brand management)
- **Cross-Functional Rotation:** Movement between departments (e.g., finance to operations)
- **Geographic Rotation:** Movement between locations (e.g., Dhaka to Chittagong offices)
- **Vertical Rotation:** Movement between hierarchical levels (e.g., temporary project leadership)

**Benefits:**
- Develops versatile, adaptable workforce
- Reduces job monotony and increases engagement
- Facilitates succession planning
- Enhances organizational communication

**Example:** **Nestle Bangladesh** operates a structured management trainee rotation program where new hires spend 3-4 months each in sales, marketing, supply chain, and finance during their first two years, building comprehensive business understanding and flexibility for future role assignments.

#### **4. Self-Managed Teams and Team-Based Work Design**

**Description:** Work structures where teams collectively share responsibility for complete work processes, with significant autonomy over how work is performed.

**Characteristics:**
- Team accountability for outcomes
- Shared decision-making authority
- Peer coordination and mutual support
- Reduced supervisory oversight
- Collective skill development

**Implementation:**
- Define clear team goals and performance metrics
- Provide training in team processes and conflict resolution
- Delegate authority for operational decisions
- Establish communication and coordination mechanisms
- Support team with resources and management coaching

**Example:** **Bangladeshi software development firms** like **Analyzen** and **Brain Station 23** organize engineers into "scrum teams" for agile software development. Teams self-organize around project goals, collectively managing sprint planning, code reviews, testing, and deployment without traditional task assignments to individuals.

#### **5. Modular and Project-Based Organization**

**Description:** Organizing work into discrete projects or modules with temporary teams assembled based on required competencies rather than permanent job assignments.

**Structure:**
- Employees have "home base" functional areas
- Project assignments based on skills and availability
- Multiple simultaneous project involvement
- Matrix reporting (functional and project managers)
- Flexible resource allocation

**Benefits:**
- Rapid response to opportunities and challenges
- Efficient use of specialized expertise
- Continuous learning through project diversity
- Clear accountability for outcomes

**Example:** **ACI Limited** (Advanced Chemical Industries) manages new product development through cross-functional project teams drawing from R&D, marketing, production, regulatory affairs, and supply chain. A pharmaceutical chemist might simultaneously work on three different drug development projects, with responsibilities defined by project needs rather than fixed job descriptions.

#### **6. Contingent Workforce Integration**

**Description:** Strategic use of non-permanent workers (consultants, contractors, freelancers, gig workers) alongside core employees to build workforce flexibility.

**Approaches:**
- Vendor management systems for contractor relationships
- Blended workforce planning (core vs. contingent)
- Project-based hiring for specialized expertise
- Flexible staffing for demand fluctuations
- Knowledge transfer mechanisms

**Considerations:**
- Legal and regulatory compliance (employment classification)
- Intellectual property protection
- Cultural integration and team cohesion
- Performance management approaches
- Compensation equity

**Example:** **Robi Axiata Limited** manages network expansion and technology upgrades through a combination of permanent network engineers and specialized contractors for specific implementations (5G rollout, fiber deployment). This allows them to scale technical capacity without permanent headcount commitments for project-specific expertise.

#### **7. Competency-Based Performance Management**

**Description:** Performance evaluation systems that assess employees against defined competencies and behavioral standards rather than task completion checklists.

**Components:**
- Competency-based goal setting
- Behavioral observation and feedback
- 360-degree evaluation incorporating multiple perspectives
- Development-oriented performance discussions
- Competency gap analysis and development planning

**Benefits:**
- Evaluates "how" work is performed, not just "what" is achieved
- Supports continuous feedback culture
- Links individual development to organizational needs
- Provides consistency across diverse roles

**Example:** **Unilever Bangladesh** uses competency-based performance management where managers evaluate employees on both "what" (business results against objectives) and "how" (demonstration of leadership competencies such as "Consumer Love," "Purpose-Led Growth," and "Bias for Action"), ensuring performance discussions transcend specific task accomplishments.

#### **8. Career Lattices vs. Career Ladders**

**Description:** Replacing traditional vertical career progression (ladder) with multi-directional career paths (lattice) that include lateral, diagonal, and flexible movements.

**Career Lattice Characteristics:**
- Multiple career paths within organization
- Lateral moves valued as developmental
- Temporary assignments and special projects
- Return-to-work and reduced-hour options
- Portfolio careers with diverse experiences

**Benefits:**
- Retains talent by offering diverse opportunities
- Develops well-rounded leaders
- Accommodates different life stages and priorities
- Utilizes organizational talent more fully

**Example:** **Marico Bangladesh Limited** encourages career lattice movements where a successful sales manager might move laterally to supply chain or marketing roles to build business breadth before advancing to country leadership, recognizing that cross-functional experience creates more effective leaders than single-function vertical progression.

#### **9. Skills Inventories and Talent Marketplaces**

**Description:** Systematic documentation of employee skills, competencies, and interests, combined with internal platforms that match employees to opportunities.

**Components:**
- Comprehensive skills databases
- Employee self-assessment and manager validation
- Internal job posting and gig platforms
- AI-matching of skills to opportunities
- Transparent opportunity marketplace

**Benefits:**
- Optimizes internal talent deployment
- Increases employee engagement and retention
- Reduces external hiring costs
- Builds organizational capability visibility

**Example:** **bKash** implemented an internal talent marketplace platform where employees can indicate interest in cross-functional projects, temporary assignments, or learning opportunities. When new initiatives arise (e.g., merchant payment system expansion), project leaders can search the talent database for employees with relevant competencies, enabling flexible talent allocation.

#### **10. Agile HR Practices**

**Description:** Applying agile principles (originally from software development) to HR processes, emphasizing iteration, feedback, adaptation, and continuous improvement.

**Agile HR Characteristics:**
- Frequent check-ins replace annual reviews
- Goal-setting is iterative (OKRs - Objectives and Key Results)
- Cross-functional collaboration on HR initiatives
- Rapid experimentation and learning
- Employee feedback loops

**Implementation:**
- Sprint-based HR projects
- Stand-up meetings for HR teams
- Retrospectives to continuously improve processes
- User-centric design of HR programs
- Data-driven decision making

**Example:** **Pathao** applies agile HR practices including quarterly OKR cycles rather than annual goals, bi-weekly one-on-ones between employees and managers for continuous feedback, and rapid experimentation with new benefits and policies based on employee input through regular pulse surveys.

## 3.7 Balancing Traditional and Modern Approaches

While the trend toward flexible, competency-based approaches is clear, organizations must balance innovation with practicality:

### **When Traditional Job Analysis Remains Appropriate:**

- **Highly regulated industries** requiring detailed documentation (banking, pharmaceuticals)
- **Manufacturing and operations roles** with standardized, repetitive tasks
- **Unionized environments** with collective bargaining agreements tied to job classifications
- **Entry-level positions** where clear expectations support new employee success
- **Legal compliance** requirements (equal employment, compensation, safety)

### **When Modern Approaches Are Essential:**

- **Knowledge work and professional roles** requiring creativity and judgment
- **Rapidly changing business environments** with frequent strategic pivots
- **Innovation-focused organizations** requiring continuous learning
- **Start-ups and growth companies** with evolving business models
- **Leadership and executive positions** requiring strategic agility

### **Integrated Approach:**

Most organizations benefit from a **hybrid model** that:
- Maintains job descriptions for regulatory compliance and basic structure
- Emphasizes competencies for selection, development, and performance management
- Allows role flexibility within documented boundaries
- Regularly updates documentation to reflect evolving work
- Balances organizational needs with employee expectations

**Example:** **Southeast Bank** maintains traditional job descriptions for compliance and compensation administration while simultaneously using competency profiles for leadership development, succession planning, and performance evaluation, demonstrating that traditional and modern approaches can coexist effectively.

## 3.11 Case Study: Transforming Job Analysis at TechBangla Solutions

**Background:**

TechBangla Solutions is a mid-sized software development company based in Dhaka, employing 250 people. Founded in 2010, the company has grown rapidly, providing custom software solutions, mobile app development, and IT consulting services to local and international clients.

For years, TechBangla maintained detailed, traditional job descriptions for all positionsâ€”from junior programmers to project managers. However, by 2023, leadership noticed several problems:

- **High turnover** among talented developers who felt constrained by rigid job definitions
- **Difficulty in adapting** to new technologies and client demands due to "that's not in my job description" mentality
- **Slow response** to market opportunities requiring cross-functional collaboration
- **Recruitment challenges** as job postings with exhaustive technical requirements attracted fewer applicants
- **Performance evaluation conflicts** as actual work had evolved beyond documented job responsibilities

The CEO commissioned the HR Director, Fatima Rahman, to reimagine how TechBangla approaches job analysis and work organization.

**Fatima's Diagnosis:**

After conducting employee focus groups, management interviews, and reviewing industry best practices, Fatima identified key issues:

1. **Outdated job descriptions** that hadn't been updated in 3-5 years despite rapid technological change
2. **Task-focused documentation** that didn't capture the creative, problem-solving nature of software development
3. **Rigid boundaries** between roles that hindered collaboration and knowledge sharing
4. **Limited career mobility** within the organization due to narrow job definitions
5. **Mismatch between evaluation criteria** and actual value creation

**The Transformation Initiative:**

Fatima proposed a comprehensive transformation with three phases:

**Phase 1: Competency Framework Development (3 months)**
- Identified core competencies for all employees (e.g., "Client Focus," "Continuous Learning," "Collaborative Problem-Solving")
- Defined functional competencies for major job families (Software Development, Project Management, Quality Assurance, Business Analysis)
- Established leadership competencies for management track
- Created technical competency library for various technologies and platforms

**Phase 2: Role Redesign (4 months)**
- Replaced 47 detailed job descriptions with 12 broad "role families"
- Documented role families with general responsibilities while emphasizing expected outcomes
- Developed competency profiles for each role family showing required vs. preferred competencies
- Created "role flexibility zones" where employees could take on additional responsibilities based on interests and project needs

**Phase 3: Supporting System Implementation (5 months)**
- Implemented internal talent marketplace where employees could indicate interests and availability for different projects
- Introduced OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework for goal-setting, replacing task-based performance metrics
- Launched competency-based learning and development programs
- Revised compensation structure with broad banding to support lateral movement
- Established quarterly "role reflection" discussions between employees and managers

**Challenges and Resistance:**

The transformation faced several obstacles:

- **Some senior managers** resisted loss of control associated with flexible roles
- **Finance department** struggled with how to maintain salary equity without traditional job grades
- **Legal concerns** about employment contract implications and potential disputes
- **Employee anxiety** about performance evaluation criteria and career progression
- **Recruitment team** worried about how to screen candidates without detailed job requirements

Fatima addressed these through:
- Executive coaching and change management workshops
- Pilot programs with willing departments before company-wide rollout
- Clear communication about benefits for employees (more autonomy, diverse experiences, faster learning)
- Legal consultation ensuring compliance while enabling flexibility
- Training for managers on competency-based interviewing and performance discussions

**Results After 18 Months:**

**Quantitative Outcomes:**
- Voluntary turnover decreased from 28% to 16% annually
- Average time-to-fill positions reduced from 65 to 42 days
- Employee engagement scores increased from 6.8 to 8.2 (out of 10)
- Cross-functional project participation increased by 150%
- Internal mobility (employees changing roles) increased by 200%

**Qualitative Outcomes:**
- Greater innovation as developers experimented with new technologies
- Faster response to client needs with flexible team configurations
- Enhanced collaboration and knowledge sharing across teams
- More diverse and inclusive hiring as qualification requirements became less rigid
- Improved employer brand attracting higher-quality candidates
