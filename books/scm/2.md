## 2.1 The Role of Distribution in the Supply Chain

### 2.1.1 What is Distribution?

**Distribution** refers to the steps taken to move and store products from the supplier stage to the customer stage in a supply chain. It represents the physical fulfillment of customer demand and is often called the "last mile" of the supply chain—though it encompasses much more.

**Formal Definition:**

> *"Distribution is the process of making a product or service available for use or consumption by a consumer or business user, involving transportation, warehousing, inventory management, order fulfillment, and information systems."*

### 2.1.2 Strategic Importance

Distribution networks directly impact two critical dimensions of supply chain performance:

**1. Customer Experience**

- **Product availability:** Can customers find what they want?
- **Delivery speed:** How quickly can products reach customers?
- **Convenience:** How easy is it for customers to access products?
- **Order visibility:** Can customers track their orders?

**2. Cost Efficiency**

- **Transportation costs:** Moving products from factories to customers
- **Inventory costs:** Holding stock at various locations
- **Facility costs:** Operating warehouses and distribution centers
- **Information costs:** Systems to manage the network

### 2.1.3 The Distribution Dilemma

Companies face a fundamental trade-off:

```
HIGH SERVICE LEVEL                    LOW COST
      ↓                                   ↓
Many facilities                    Few facilities
High inventory                     Low inventory
Fast delivery                      Slower delivery
High availability                  Basic availability
      ↓                                   ↓
    HIGH COST                      LOW SERVICE LEVEL
```

**The Challenge:** Design a distribution network that balances customer service with cost efficiency to maximize profitability.

### 2.1.4 Distribution's Impact on Business Performance

| Performance Metric | Distribution Impact | Example |
|-------------------|---------------------|---------|
| **Revenue** | Better availability increases sales | Amazon Prime's 2-day delivery increases purchase frequency by 25% |
| **Market Share** | Superior distribution creates competitive advantage | Coca-Cola's extensive distribution reaches 99% of retail outlets globally |
| **Customer Loyalty** | Reliable delivery builds trust | Zappos' exceptional delivery service generates 75% repeat customers |
| **Operating Costs** | Distribution represents 5-15% of revenue | Walmart's distribution efficiency saves $1 billion+ annually |
| **Working Capital** | Inventory positioning affects cash flow | Dell's direct model reduced inventory days from 40 to 4 |

## 2.2 Factors Influencing Distribution Network Design

Designing an effective distribution network requires careful analysis of multiple factors. These factors can be grouped into strategic, market, product, and operational considerations.

### 2.2.1 Strategic Factors

#### **1. Competitive Strategy**

Different strategies require different distribution approaches:

| Strategy | Distribution Requirements | Example |
|----------|--------------------------|---------|
| **Cost Leadership** | Minimize distribution costs, centralized facilities | Walmart's regional distribution centers |
| **Differentiation** | Maximum availability, faster delivery | Apple's retail stores + online presence |
| **Focus/Niche** | Specialized channels, personalized service | Luxury brands with exclusive boutiques |

#### **2. Firm's Capabilities**

**Internal Assessment:**
- Financial resources for facility investment
- Technological capabilities for network management
- Expertise in logistics and distribution
- Risk tolerance for network complexity

**Strategic Decisions:**
- **Ownership:** Company-owned vs. outsourced facilities
- **Control:** Direct management vs. third-party logistics (3PL)
- **Integration:** Vertical integration vs. partnerships

#### **3. Growth Projections**

Networks must accommodate future growth:

- **Scalability:** Can facilities handle increased volume?
- **Flexibility:** Can the network adapt to new markets?
- **Modularity:** Can capacity be added incrementally?

**Planning Horizon:** Distribution facilities represent 10-20 year investments, requiring long-term demand forecasting.

### 2.2.2 Market Factors

#### **1. Customer Density and Geographic Spread**

**High Density Markets:**
- Support multiple local facilities
- Enable shorter delivery times
- Justify higher facility costs

**Low Density Markets:**
- Require centralized distribution
- Longer delivery times acceptable
- Transportation costs dominate

**Illustration:**

```
URBAN MARKET (High Density)          RURAL MARKET (Low Density)
     Multiple DCs                          Single DC
         ↓                                     ↓
    Short distances                      Long distances
         ↓                                     ↓
    Fast, frequent                       Slower, batched
    deliveries                           deliveries
```

#### **2. Customer Service Expectations**

Modern customers demand:

| Expectation | Distribution Implication |
|-------------|-------------------------|
| **Fast delivery** | Facilities closer to customers |
| **Same-day/next-day** | Local fulfillment centers |
| **Real-time tracking** | Advanced IT systems |
| **Flexible delivery options** | Multiple channel integration |
| **Easy returns** | Reverse logistics capability |

**Example:** Urban millennials expect 2-day delivery as standard, while rural customers may accept 5-7 days.

#### **3. Market Size and Growth Rate**

**Large, Growing Markets:**
- Justify dedicated distribution infrastructure
- Support specialized facilities
- Enable economies of scale

**Small, Mature Markets:**
- Require shared facilities
- Favor flexible, multi-client solutions
- Need cost minimization focus

### 2.2.3 Product Characteristics

#### **1. Product Value**

**High-Value Products** (electronics, jewelry):
- Centralized storage acceptable
- Air freight economically viable
- Security concerns paramount
- Lower inventory at retail

**Low-Value Products** (groceries, beverages):
- Decentralized storage necessary
- Ground transportation required
- High inventory turnover needed
- Wide retail availability essential

#### **2. Product Size and Weight**

**Large/Heavy Products** (furniture, appliances):
- High transportation costs
- Regional warehouses preferred
- Direct-to-customer shipping expensive
- Retail showrooms without inventory

**Small/Light Products** (books, clothing):
- Lower transportation costs
- Centralized distribution feasible
- E-commerce friendly
- Multiple distribution options

#### **3. Product Variety**

**High Variety:**
- Centralized storage reduces total inventory
- Specialized facilities for different categories
- Risk pooling benefits
- Complex forecasting requirements

**Low Variety:**
- Decentralized storage feasible
- Simpler inventory management
- Predictable demand patterns
- Economies of scale in storage

#### **4. Perishability and Shelf Life**

| Product Type | Shelf Life | Distribution Strategy |
|-------------|------------|----------------------|
| **Highly perishable** | Hours to days | Local production, rapid distribution |
| **Perishable** | Weeks | Regional facilities, cold chain |
| **Semi-perishable** | Months | Standard distribution, rotation management |
| **Non-perishable** | Years | Centralized, cost-optimized |

**Examples:**
- Fresh fish: 24-48 hours → Daily distribution from source
- Dairy: 2-3 weeks → Regional cold storage
- Canned goods: 2+ years → Centralized distribution

### 2.2.4 Operational Factors

#### **1. Transportation Options and Costs**

**Mode Selection:**

| Mode | Speed | Cost | Best For |
|------|-------|------|----------|
| **Air** | Fastest | Highest | High-value, urgent |
| **Truck** | Fast | Medium | Flexible, door-to-door |
| **Rail** | Medium | Low | Bulk, long distance |
| **Ship** | Slow | Lowest | Heavy, international |

**Network Implications:**
- Good road infrastructure → Decentralized distribution
- Limited transportation → Centralized hubs
- High fuel costs → Fewer, larger shipments

#### **2. Facility Costs**

**Cost Components:**

```
FACILITY COSTS
    ↓
├── Land/Building (Fixed)
├── Equipment (Forklifts, racking, systems)
├── Labor (Warehouse workers, managers)
├── Utilities (Power, climate control)
├── Maintenance
└── Technology (WMS, automation)
```

**Trade-offs:**
- **Many small facilities:** Higher total facility costs, lower transportation
- **Few large facilities:** Lower facility costs, higher transportation

#### **3. Information Technology Capabilities**

**Essential Systems:**

- **Warehouse Management System (WMS):** Inventory control, order picking
- **Transportation Management System (TMS):** Route optimization, carrier selection
- **Order Management System (OMS):** Order processing, tracking
- **Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP):** Integrated business processes

**Network Complexity vs. IT Requirements:**
- Simple networks → Basic systems adequate
- Complex networks → Sophisticated integration essential

#### **4. Inventory Considerations**

**Centralized Inventory:**

**Advantages:**
- Lower total inventory (risk pooling)
- Better forecasting accuracy
- Simplified management

**Disadvantages:**
- Longer delivery times
- Higher transportation costs
- Single point of failure

**Decentralized Inventory:**

**Advantages:**
- Faster customer response
- Lower transportation costs
- Regional customization

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher total inventory
- Complex coordination
- Risk of imbalances

## 2.3 Distribution Network Design Options

Companies can choose from several fundamental distribution network designs, each with distinct characteristics, advantages, and trade-offs.

### 2.3.1 Manufacturer Storage with Direct Shipping

#### **Structure:**

```
MANUFACTURER → [Direct Shipment] → CUSTOMER
    └── All inventory held at manufacturing facility
```

#### **How It Works:**

1. Customer places order (typically online)
2. Order transmitted to manufacturer
3. Product picked, packed, and shipped directly from factory
4. Delivery to customer's location

#### **Characteristics:**

- **Inventory Location:** All held at manufacturing site
- **Delivery Time:** Typically 5-10 days
- **Transportation:** Individual shipments to customers
- **Examples:** Dell computers (1990s-2000s), custom furniture manufacturers

#### **Advantages:**

| Advantage | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Lowest inventory costs** | Single inventory location, no pipeline inventory |
| **High product variety** | Can offer full product range without retail space constraints |
| **Centralized quality control** | All products inspected at source |
| **Lower facility costs** | No separate distribution centers needed |
| **Easy customization** | Products built/customized to order |

#### **Disadvantages:**

| Disadvantage | Impact |
|--------------|--------|
| **Long delivery times** | Customer must wait for shipment from factory |
| **High transportation costs** | Many small, individual shipments (no consolidation) |
| **Complex coordination** | Multiple shipments to same area not consolidated |
| **Limited geographic reach** | International shipping expensive |
| **Visibility challenges** | Difficult to provide accurate delivery estimates |

#### **Best Suited For:**

- High-value, low-demand products
- Highly customizable products
- Products with high variety
- Markets with patient customers
- Digital/downloadable products

### 2.3.2 Manufacturer Storage with Direct Shipping and In-Transit Merge

#### **Structure:**

```
MANUFACTURER A ──┐
MANUFACTURER B ──┼── [Merge Point] ──→ CUSTOMER
MANUFACTURER C ──┘
```

#### **How It Works:**

1. Customer orders multiple products from different manufacturers
2. Each manufacturer ships to a merge facility
3. Products consolidated into single shipment
4. Unified delivery to customer

#### **Characteristics:**

- **Inventory Location:** At multiple manufacturers
- **Merge Point:** Third-party logistics provider
- **Delivery Time:** 5-7 days
- **Example:** Computer systems with components from different suppliers

#### **Advantages:**

- Reduced transportation costs through consolidation
- Single delivery for multiple products
- Lower inventory than distributed storage
- Better customer experience than separate deliveries

#### **Disadvantages:**

- Added complexity of coordination
- Merge facility costs
- Delays if one component is late
- Limited to compatible product combinations

#### **Best Suited For:**

- Systems with components from multiple manufacturers
- High-value products justifying merge costs
- B2B orders with multiple SKUs

### 2.3.3 Distributor Storage with Carrier Delivery

#### **Structure:**

```
MANUFACTURER → DISTRIBUTOR/DC → [Carrier] → CUSTOMER
                    ↓
              (Inventory held here)
```

#### **How It Works:**

1. Manufacturer ships in bulk to distributor/DC
2. Distributor holds inventory
3. Customer places order
4. Distributor picks, packs, ships via carrier
5. Carrier delivers to customer

#### **Characteristics:**

- **Inventory Location:** Regional distribution centers
- **Delivery Time:** 2-5 days
- **Transportation:** Carrier-based (UPS, FedEx, local couriers)
- **Examples:** Amazon fulfillment centers, traditional e-commerce

#### **Advantages:**

| Advantage | Business Impact |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Faster delivery** | Proximity to customers reduces transit time |
| **Lower transportation costs** | Bulk shipments to DC, zone-based shipping |
| **Better inventory availability** | Products ready to ship immediately |
| **Carrier expertise** | Professional delivery services |
| **Returns management** | Easier to handle returns regionally |

#### **Disadvantages:**

| Disadvantage | Challenge |
|--------------|-----------|
| **Higher inventory costs** | Inventory at multiple locations |
| **Facility costs** | Investment in distribution centers |
| **Inventory imbalances** | Some DCs may stock out while others have excess |
| **Forecasting complexity** | Must predict demand by region |
| **Coordination challenges** | Multiple facilities to manage |

#### **Best Suited For:**

- Medium-value products with reasonable demand
- Products with moderate variety
- Markets expecting fast delivery
- Established e-commerce businesses
- Products with predictable demand patterns

**Optimal Configuration:**

```
NUMBER OF DCs vs. COST/SERVICE

            |    Total Cost
            |        ___/‾‾‾
            |      _/
            |    _/  Transportation
            |  _/    Cost
            | /
            |/________
            |         ‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾
            | Facility + Inventory Cost
            |________________________
              1   2   3   4   5  DCs

Service     |
Level       |            ___________
            |          _/
            |        _/
            |      _/
            |    _/
            |  _/
            | /
            |_________________________
              1   2   3   4   5  DCs
```

### 2.3.4 Distributor Storage with Last-Mile Delivery

#### **Structure:**

```
MANUFACTURER → DISTRIBUTOR/DC → [Company Fleet] → CUSTOMER
                    ↓              (Last-mile)
              (Inventory held here)
```

#### **How It Works:**

1. Distributor holds inventory regionally
2. Customer places order
3. Distributor consolidates multiple orders
4. Company-owned fleet delivers directly to homes/businesses
5. Scheduled delivery windows

#### **Characteristics:**

- **Inventory Location:** Regional distribution centers
- **Delivery Time:** Same-day to 2 days
- **Transportation:** Company-owned delivery fleet
- **Examples:** Grocery delivery (Instacart, local grocers), meal kits, bottled water

#### **Advantages:**

| Advantage | Value Proposition |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Fastest delivery** | Same-day or next-day possible |
| **Scheduled deliveries** | Customer convenience, time windows |
| **Multiple orders consolidated** | Efficient route planning |
| **Product handling control** | Quality maintained throughout |
| **Customer interaction** | Direct relationship building |
| **Reverse logistics** | Easy returns/exchanges on delivery |

#### **Disadvantages:**

| Disadvantage | Cost Impact |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Highest transportation cost** | Dedicated fleet expensive |
| **Fleet management complexity** | Vehicles, drivers, routes, maintenance |
| **Geographic limitations** | Only economical in dense areas |
| **Utilization challenges** | Fleet capacity must match peak demand |
| **Labor costs** | Driver wages, benefits |

#### **Best Suited For:**

- Perishable products requiring careful handling
- High-frequency purchases (groceries, meals)
- Dense urban markets
- Products benefiting from customer interaction
- Subscription-based models

**Economic Viability Analysis:**

```
DELIVERY DENSITY THRESHOLD

Profitable   ┤                    ___/‾‾‾‾
             ┤                 __/
Break-even   ┤              __/
             ┤           __/
Loss         ┤        __/
             ┤     __/
             ┤  __/
             └─────────────────────────────
               Low      Medium     High
                 Customer Density
```

**Minimum Viability:** Typically requires 50+ deliveries per day per route to be cost-effective.

### 2.3.5 Manufacturer/Distributor Storage with Customer Pickup

#### **Structure:**

```
MANUFACTURER/DISTRIBUTOR → PICKUP LOCATION → CUSTOMER
           ↓                     ↑           (self-pickup)
    (Inventory held)        (Customer travels)
```

#### **How It Works:**

1. Customer orders online or in-store
2. Order fulfilled at warehouse/store
3. Customer notified when ready
4. Customer travels to pickup location
5. Order collected at convenient time

#### **Characteristics:**

- **Inventory Location:** Stores, lockers, or dedicated pickup points
- **Delivery Time:** Same-day to 2 days
- **Transportation:** Customer bears last-mile cost
- **Examples:** Retail click-and-collect, Amazon Lockers, BOPIS (Buy Online Pick up In Store)

#### **Advantages:**

| Advantage | Benefit |
|-----------|---------|
| **Lowest delivery cost** | Customer handles last mile |
| **Fast availability** | Same-day pickup often possible |
| **Reduced missed deliveries** | No home delivery failures |
| **Flexibility** | Customer picks up at convenience |
| **Additional sales opportunity** | In-store pickup drives impulse purchases |
| **Lower return rates** | Customer inspects product at pickup |

#### **Disadvantages:**

| Disadvantage | Customer Impact |
|--------------|-----------------|
| **Customer inconvenience** | Must travel to location |
| **Limited hours** | Pickup location hours may not suit all |
| **Geographic coverage** | Requires nearby locations |
| **Customer acceptance** | Not suitable for all product types |
| **Space requirements** | Storage for pickup orders |

#### **Best Suited For:**

- Retailers with existing store networks
- Urban/suburban areas with store density
- Products customers want immediately
- Cost-conscious customers
- Items requiring inspection before acceptance

**Hybrid Models:**

Many retailers offer multiple options:

```
CUSTOMER ORDER
      ↓
   OPTIONS:
      ├── Home Delivery (fee)
      ├── Store Pickup (free)
      ├── Locker Pickup (free)
      └── Curbside Pickup (free)
```

### 2.3.6 Retail Storage with Customer Pickup

#### **Structure:**

```
MANUFACTURER → DISTRIBUTOR → RETAIL STORE → CUSTOMER
                                  ↓         (walks in)
                           (Inventory on floor)
```

#### **How It Works:**

1. Retailer forecasts demand and orders inventory
2. Products stored and displayed in retail stores
3. Customer visits store
4. Customer browses, selects, purchases immediately
5. Customer transports product home

#### **Characteristics:**

- **Inventory Location:** Retail store shelves
- **Delivery Time:** Immediate
- **Transportation:** Customer handles completely
- **Examples:** Supermarkets, department stores, traditional retail

#### **Advantages:**

| Advantage | Customer Value |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Immediate availability** | Customer gets product now |
| **Touch and feel** | Physical product inspection |
| **No shipping costs** | Customer transports |
| **No delivery wait** | Instant gratification |
| **Impulse purchases** | Browsing leads to additional sales |
| **Personal assistance** | Sales staff available |

#### **Disadvantages:**

| Disadvantage | Retailer Challenge |
|--------------|--------------------|
| **Highest inventory costs** | Inventory at all store locations |
| **Highest facility costs** | Prime retail real estate expensive |
| **Stock-outs** | Lost sales when products unavailable |
| **Inventory imbalances** | Some stores overstock, others stock out |
| **Limited variety** | Physical space constraints |
| **Geographic coverage** | Customer must be near store |

#### **Best Suited For:**

- Products customers want immediately
- Products requiring examination
- Impulse purchase products
- Low-value, high-turnover items
- Markets with limited e-commerce adoption

**Optimization Strategies:**

1. **Efficient replenishment:** Frequent, small deliveries to stores
2. **Inventory visibility:** Real-time tracking across stores
3. **Store clustering:** Regional distribution supports store groups
4. **Assortment planning:** Right products for each location

### 2.3.7 Comparative Analysis of Design Options

| Design Option | Inventory Cost | Facility Cost | Transport Cost | Delivery Speed | Best For |
|---------------|----------------|---------------|----------------|----------------|----------|
| **Manufacturer Direct** | Lowest | Lowest | Highest | Slowest | Customized products |
| **In-Transit Merge** | Low | Low | High | Slow | Multi-component systems |
| **Distributor + Carrier** | Medium | Medium | Medium | Medium | Standard e-commerce |
| **Distributor + Last Mile** | Medium | Medium | Highest | Fastest | Perishables, groceries |
| **Customer Pickup** | Medium | Low | Lowest | Fast | Cost-conscious customers |
| **Retail Storage** | Highest | Highest | Low | Immediate | Impulse, immediate need |

### 2.3.8 Selecting the Right Design

**Decision Framework:**

```
START: Analyze Product & Market
         ↓
    HIGH VALUE? ────YES──→ Consider Manufacturer Direct
         ↓ NO
    FAST DELIVERY CRITICAL? ────YES──→ Local Storage Required
         ↓ NO                              ↓
    CUSTOMER DENSITY? ────HIGH──→ Last-Mile Delivery
         ↓ LOW                    ↓ MEDIUM
    CUSTOMIZATION? ────YES──→ Distributor + Carrier
         ↓ NO                     ↓
    Retail Storage            Store Pickup
```

**Hybrid Approaches:**

Most successful companies use **multiple designs simultaneously:**

- **By product type:** Fast-moving items in stores, slow-moving via distributor
- **By geography:** Dense urban areas get last-mile, rural gets carrier
- **By customer segment:** Premium customers get home delivery, value customers pickup
- **By channel:** Online orders from DC, walk-ins from stores

## 2.4 Impact of e-Business on Distribution Networks

The rise of e-business has fundamentally transformed distribution network design, creating new opportunities and challenges.

### 2.4.1 The e-Business Revolution

#### **Definition:**

**e-Business** refers to the use of digital platforms and the internet to conduct business transactions, encompassing e-commerce (buying/selling), e-procurement, digital marketing, and online customer service.

#### **Growth Statistics:**

- Global e-commerce sales: $5.8 trillion (2023), projected $8 trillion (2027)
- E-commerce as percentage of retail: 20% globally, 30%+ in developed markets
- Mobile commerce: 60% of e-commerce transactions
- Cross-border e-commerce: Growing 25% annually

### 2.4.2 Fundamental Changes to Distribution

#### **1. Disintermediation vs. Reintermediation**

**Disintermediation:** Elimination of intermediaries

```
TRADITIONAL:
Manufacturer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Customer

E-BUSINESS:
Manufacturer → Customer
```

**Example:** Dell's direct-to-consumer model eliminated retail markup

**Reintermediation:** New intermediaries emerge

```
E-BUSINESS REALITY:
Manufacturer → E-commerce Platform → Customer
               (Amazon, Alibaba)
```

**Example:** Third-party sellers on Amazon reach customers through the platform's distribution network

#### **2. From Push to Pull**

**Traditional Retail (Push):**
- Forecast-driven inventory
- Products pushed to stores
- Limited variety in stores
- High inventory costs

**E-Business (More Pull):**
- Order-driven fulfillment
- Products held centrally until ordered
- Unlimited virtual variety
- Lower inventory per SKU

**Hybrid Reality:**

```
POPULAR ITEMS (80% of volume)
    ↓
PUSHED to regional DCs
    ↓
Fast delivery possible

LONG-TAIL ITEMS (20% of volume)
    ↓
PULLED from central warehouse
    ↓
Slower delivery acceptable
```

#### **3. Inventory Aggregation Benefits**

**The Square Root Law:**

When consolidating N locations into 1 central location, total inventory reduces by approximately √N.

**Example:**

```
BEFORE: 100 retail stores, each holding 100 units = 10,000 units total

AFTER: 1 central DC holding 10,000/√100 = 1,000 units

INVENTORY REDUCTION: 90%
```

**Real-world caveat:** Actual reduction is 40-60% due to increased demand variability and longer lead times.

### 2.4.3 New Distribution Challenges

#### **1. Last-Mile Complexity**

**The Problem:**

Last-mile delivery (from distribution center to customer's door) represents:
- 53% of total shipping costs
- Greatest source of customer dissatisfaction
- Highest environmental impact
- Most complex operational challenge

**e-Business Solutions:**

| Challenge | Solution | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **High cost** | Route optimization software | UPS's ORION saves $400M annually |
| **Missed deliveries** | Smart lockers, pickup points | Amazon Lockers, 7-Eleven partnerships |
| **Urban congestion** | Micro-fulfillment centers | Dark stores in city centers |
| **Customer expectations** | Delivery time windows | Evening/weekend delivery options |
| **Returns** | Simplified reverse logistics | Free returns, doorstep pickup |

#### **2. The Expectations Escalation**

**Customer Expectations Evolution:**

```
2000: 7-10 days acceptable
2010: 3-5 days standard
2015: 2-day delivery (Prime effect)
2020: Next-day, same-day expected
2025: 1-hour delivery in urban areas
```

**Distribution Response:**

- **Urban micro-fulfillment:** Small warehouses in cities
- **Predictive positioning:** AI predicts demand, pre-positions inventory
- **Crowdsourced delivery:** Uber, Instacart shoppers
- **Autonomous delivery:** Drones, robots in testing

#### **3. Omnichannel Integration

**Definition:**

Omnichannel is a seamless customer experience across all channels (online, mobile, in-store), with unified inventory and fulfillment.

**Customer Journey Example:**

```
1. RESEARCH online (mobile app)
      ↓
2. CHECK in-store availability (website)
      ↓
3. ORDER online for store pickup (laptop)
      ↓
4. PICKUP in store
      ↓
5. RETURN via mail (different item than purchased)
```

**Distribution Requirements:**

- **Unified inventory visibility:** Real-time stock across all channels
- **Flexible fulfillment:** Ship from store, deliver to store, transfer between stores
- **Consistent pricing:** Same prices regardless of channel
- **Integrated systems:** Single platform for all channels

**Ship-from-Store:**

```
TRADITIONAL:           OMNICHANNEL:
Customer → DC → Store  Customer → Nearest Store
                            (ships directly)
```

**Benefits:**
- Faster delivery (store closer than DC)
- Reduced DC load
- Better inventory utilization
- Converts stores into micro-fulfillment centers

**Challenges:**
- Store staff must fulfill online orders
- Inventory accuracy critical
- Cannibalization concerns
- Systems integration complexity

### 2.4.4 Technology Enablers

#### **1. Warehouse Automation**

**Technologies:**

| Technology | Function | Benefit |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Automated Storage/Retrieval (AS/RS)** | High-density storage, robotic picking | 3x inventory density, 99.9% accuracy |
| **Goods-to-Person systems** | Robots bring products to pickers | 2-3x productivity |
| **Automated sorting** | High-speed package sorting | 10,000+ packages/hour |
| **Automated packing** | Right-sized boxes, automated sealing | 40% packaging material reduction |

**Example:** Amazon's Kiva robots reduce order fulfillment time from 60-75 minutes to 15 minutes.

#### **2. Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning**

**Applications:**

**Demand Forecasting:**
- Predicts demand by location, time, SKU
- Accuracy improvement: 20-50%
- Enables optimal inventory positioning

**Route Optimization:**
- Real-time traffic consideration
- Dynamic route adjustments
- Fuel savings: 10-15%

**Inventory Allocation:**
- Determines which DC fulfills each order
- Minimizes total cost and delivery time
- Reduces split shipments

#### **3. Internet of Things (IoT)**

**Use Cases:**

- **Real-time tracking:** GPS on every package, truck, container
- **Condition monitoring:** Temperature, humidity for perishables
- **Predictive maintenance:** Equipment monitors itself
- **Automated replenishment:** Smart shelves detect low stock

**Impact on Distribution:**

- Full supply chain visibility
- Proactive exception management
- Quality assurance throughout chain
- Data for continuous improvement

#### **4. Blockchain**

**Distribution Applications:**

- **Traceability:** Complete product history from origin to customer
- **Authenticity:** Prevents counterfeits
- **Smart contracts:** Automated payment upon delivery confirmation
- **Multi-party coordination:** Shared, trusted data across partners

**Example:** Walmart tracks produce from farm to store in 2.2 seconds (vs. 7 days previously) using blockchain.

### 2.4.5 Environmental Considerations

#### **e-Commerce Environmental Impact:**

**Negative Impacts:**
- More packaging waste (individual shipments)
- More delivery vehicles on roads
- Failed deliveries waste resources
- Returns double transportation

**Positive Impacts:**
- Reduced retail space (energy, materials)
- Optimized routing reduces miles
- Consolidated shipping from DCs
- Reduced customer driving to stores

**Net Impact:** Varies by product, density, and system design.

#### **Sustainable Distribution Strategies:**

| Strategy | Implementation | Impact |
|----------|----------------|--------|
| **Electric delivery vehicles** | Amazon's 100,000 EV order | 40% emissions reduction |
| **Consolidated delivery** | Combine multiple orders | Fewer vehicles needed |

## 2.6 Distribution Channels of Agricultural Products in Bangladesh

Agriculture employs 40% of Bangladesh's workforce and contributes 11% to GDP. The distribution of agricultural products involves complex, multi-layered channels connecting millions of small farmers to consumers.

### 2.6.1 Traditional Agricultural Supply Chain Structure

#### **Primary Participants:**

```
FARMER (1.5 crore farming households)
   ↓
FARIA (Village collector/trader)
   ↓
BEPARI (Wholesale trader)
   ↓
ARATDAR (Commission agent at wholesale market)
   ↓
PAIKAR (Distributor/Wholesaler)
   ↓
RETAILER (Local markets, street vendors)
   ↓
CONSUMER
```

#### **Detailed Channel Description:**

**1. Farmer (Krishak):**

- **Profile:** Small landholders (average 0.6 hectares)
- **Challenges:**
  - Limited storage facilities
  - Price vulnerability
  - Lack of market information
  - Immediate cash needs force early selling

**2. Faria (Village Collector):**

- **Role:** First point of aggregation
- **Function:**
  - Visits villages, purchases from multiple farmers
  - Provides immediate cash to farmers
  - Aggregates small quantities
  - Transports to local markets
- **Margin:** 5-10% typically

**3. Bepari (Wholesale Trader):**

- **Role:** Transport and bulk aggregation
- **Function:**
  - Purchases from multiple Farias
  - Arranges transportation to major wholesale markets
  - Bears transportation risk
  - May provide credit to Farias
- **Margin:** 8-12%

**4. Aratdar (Commission Agent):**

- **Role:** Facilitates transactions at wholesale markets
- **Function:**
  - Operates stall/space in wholesale market (Karwan Bazar, Shyambazar, etc.)
  - Connects sellers (Bepari) with buyers (Paikar)
  - Provides temporary storage
  - Charges commission (generally 10% of transaction value)
  - May provide credit facilities
- **Margin:** 10% commission

**5. Paikar (Distributor):**

- **Role:** Bulk buyer for distribution
- **Function:**
  - Purchases large quantities from Aratdars
  - Distributes to retailers in specific areas
  - May sort and regrade products
  - Provides credit to retailers
- **Margin:** 10-15%

**6. Retailer (Bhumikary/Pherawala):**

- **Types:**
  - **Fixed shops:** Permanent market stalls
  - **Street vendors:** Mobile sellers
  - **Neighborhood shops:** Small grocery stores
- **Function:**
  - Final point of sale to consumers
  - Small-scale selling
  - Personal relationships with customers
- **Margin:** 15-25%

#### **Channel Efficiency Analysis:**

**Value Addition by Layer:**

| Channel Layer | Price Increase | Value Added | Wastage |
|---------------|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Farm gate** | 100 (base) | Production | 5% |
| **After Faria** | 110 | Collection, initial transport | 8% |
| **After Bepari** | 125 | Aggregation, long transport | 10% |
| **After Aratdar** | 140 | Market access, temporary storage | 12% |
| **After Paikar** | 160 | Distribution, sorting | 8% |
| **Retail** | 200 | Convenience, small quantities | 5% |

**Consumer pays 2x farm gate price, with 48% cumulative wastage and margin.**

## 2.8 Distribution Networks in Practice: Bangladesh Overview

### 2.8.1 Cross-Sector Patterns

Despite differences between agriculture and FMCG, common patterns emerge:

#### **Multi-Layered Structure:**

Both sectors rely on multiple intermediaries:

```
PRODUCER → AGGREGATOR → WHOLESALER → DISTRIBUTOR → RETAILER → CONSUMER
```

**Rationale:**
- Fragmented production/small manufacturers
- Vast geography with poor infrastructure
- Need for credit extension
- Risk distribution across layers

#### **Regional Hubs:**

Major cities act as distribution hubs:

| Hub | Coverage | Key Markets |
|-----|----------|-------------|
| **Dhaka** | Central Bangladesh, national reach | Karwan Bazar, Gulshan, Mohammadpur |
| **Chattogram** | Southeast, port city | Khatunganj, Chaktai |
| **Sylhet** | Northeast | Sylhet Sadar |
| **Rajshahi** | Northwest | Rajshahi city markets |
| **Khulna** | Southwest | Khulna city markets |

#### **Infrastructure Constraints:**

- Limited cold chain
- Traffic congestion
- Inadequate warehousing
- Last-mile challenges

### 2.8.2 Infrastructure Developments Impacting Distribution

#### **1. Padma Bridge (Opened June 2022):**

**Impact:**

```
BEFORE:                          AFTER:
Dhaka → Southwest                Dhaka → Southwest
    ↓                                ↓
6-7 hours (ferry queue)          3 hours (direct)
    ↓                                ↓
Limited product range            Expanded product range
High transport cost              30% cost reduction
```

**Distribution Benefits:**
- 21 southern districts better connected
- Perishables can now reach Dhaka
- Same-day delivery possible
- Reduced logistics costs

**Example:** Fresh fish from Khulna now reaches Dhaka markets within 4 hours

#### **2. Dhaka Elevated Expressway (Partial opening 2023):**

**Benefits:**
- Faster intra-city distribution
- Reduced fuel costs
- Better delivery time reliability
- Night distribution facilitated

#### **3. Industrial Parks and Special Economic Zones:**

**Examples:**
- Karnaphuli EPZ (Chattogram)
- Mongla EPZ
- Private industrial parks (Bhaluka, Mirsarai)

**Distribution Advantages:**
- Clustered manufacturing
- Shared logistics infrastructure
- Better road connectivity
- Customs facilitation
